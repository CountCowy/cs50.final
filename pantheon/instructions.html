How to Scan Dactylic Hexameter

Introduction

Dactylic hexameter is the meter of epic Latin and Greek poetry, such as Virgil’s Aeneid or Homer’s Iliad. Each line consists of six metrical feet. The first four feet can be dactyls (– ∪ ∪) or spondees (– –). The fifth foot is usually a dactyl, and the sixth foot is a spondee.

Step 1: Divide into Syllables

Break the line into syllables and mark long (–) and short (∪) vowels. Rules:

  • Long by nature: ā, ē, ī, ō, ū, ȳ
  • Long by position: vowel followed by two consonants (or x, z)
  • Short vowels: all others
Example:
Line: Litora, multum ille et terris iactatus et alto
Syllables: li-to-ra, mul-tum il-le et ter-ris i-ac-ta-tus et al-to

Step 2: Identify Elisions

Elisions occur when an unaccented vowel at the end of a word combines with a vowel at the start of the next word.

Example: mul-tum il-le → mul-tum ille (the e of multum elides with the i of ille)

Step 3: Mark Longs and Shorts

Go syllable by syllable:

  • – : long syllable
  • ∪ : short syllable
Line: li-to-ra, mul-tum il-le et ter-ris i-ac-ta-tus et al-to
Scansion: – ∪ ∪ | – – | – ∪ ∪ | – – | – ∪ ∪ | – –

Step 4: Divide into Feet

Group the syllables into six feet:

  1. Dactyl or spondee
  2. Dactyl or spondee
  3. Dactyl or spondee
  4. Dactyl or spondee
  5. Dactyl (usually)
  6. Spondee

Summary

Check that your line follows hexameter rules:

  • First four feet: dactyl (– ∪ ∪) or spondee (– –)
  • Fifth foot: usually dactyl (– ∪ ∪)
  • Sixth foot: spondee (– –)
– : long syllable
  ∪ : short syllable
  | : foot division
  () : metrical foot

Practice is key—start with short lines and gradually scan longer epic verses.
